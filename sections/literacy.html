Financial Literacy

Why it is Important

Financial literacy underpins long‑term financial well‑being and economic health. Only 27% of adults globally are financially literate, and in the US just 35% demonstrate adequate financial knowledge [167]. Low literacy leads to poorer investment choices, higher‑cost debt, and insufficient retirement savings. By contrast, people with strong financial skills plan and save for retirement more effectively [168].

Market Size / Opportunity

Financial wellness programs: $2.33B (2024) → $2.67B (2025) [169].
Personal finance software: $1.08B (2022) → $1.59B by 2030 [170].
Workplace programs matter: 58% of U.S. employers offer them, and engaged employees are 34% more likely to increase retirement contributions [167].

Important Insights

Gender gap: Men score 63% vs women 55% on literacy; women are 33% more likely to defer decisions to a partner [167].
Age patterns: Ages 18–24 have the lowest literacy (~35.2%), peaking at 35–44 (~50.3%) before declining [167].
Education works: High performers are 72% more likely to save and 50% more likely to compare prices [171].

Success Stories

  • Wells Fargo “Hands on Banking” – Free courses from budgeting to retirement planning [172].
  • Bank of America – Erica – AI assistant delivering personalized insights and guidance [172].
  • European Banking Federation – Programs in 35 countries (European Money Week, Money Quiz) [173].
  • UNESCO–Visa Foundation – Global youth financial challenge across 35 countries [167].
  • EU curricula – Financial transparency education in high schools (from 2025) [167].

Ideas for CEE Banks

  • Regional Financial Literacy Certification: Credentialed courses that improve employability and deepen bank relationships.
  • Gamified Family Finance: Multi‑generational games, challenges, and local simulations tailored to CEE contexts.
  • SME Financial Capability: Training on cash‑flow, trade finance, and EU compliance for small businesses.
  • Digital Literacy for Seniors: Safe mobile/online banking, cybersecurity hygiene, and fraud prevention.
  • School‑to‑Bank Pipeline: Secondary‑school curriculum + internships + pathways into banking careers.
