Monetization of Internal Capabilities Through Horizontal Scaling

Turning proven internal tools and platforms into external products is emerging as a powerful growth lever in financial services. The pattern (e.g., Revolut People) shows how banks and fintechs can productize HR, onboarding, risk, KYC, fraud, and data capabilities to unlock new revenue streams while accelerating transformation.

Executive Summary

Horizontal scaling of internal capabilities—commercializing internal tools as external services—can add ~1–2% incremental revenue while strengthening competitive moats and ecosystems[12][55]. Revolut’s HR suite (Revolut People) is a live example: built for 10k+ staff, now priced at £7.99 per employee/month for >100 customers[3][4][1]. The enabling rails are API‑first architectures and BaaS markets scaling toward $98B–$238B this decade[6][9][26].

Deep Overview

The Phenomenon

Internal capability monetization commercializes in‑house tools, processes, and tech—shifting them from cost centers to revenue products[2][5]. Examples span HR (Revolut People), onboarding, risk and analytics, and observability platforms. Pricing and go‑to‑market lean on APIs, usage tiers, and partner channels, mirroring BaaS patterns[6][10].

Historical Roots

Tech precedents include Slack (ex‑internal tool), Asana, and Atlassian’s Jira, proving internal adoption as an early product‑market fit signal[5]. In banking, open‑banking regulation (PSD2→PSD3) and API‑first modernization lowered the friction to “externalize” proven capabilities[29][26].

Strategic Implications

Firms allocating ~20% of growth capital to new businesses materially outperform; most already hold underutilized assets (software, data, tooling) for spin‑outs[12]. Revolut’s horizontal move illustrates speed and capital efficiency when productizing what’s already scaled internally[3][4].

Implications for Banks

Revenue Opportunities

  • Data monetization: leading banks generate hundreds of millions; uplift often 1–2% of revenues via anonymized/aggregated data and insights[7][8][55].
  • API commercialization: BaaS/Open‑Banking models with per‑call, tiered, or revenue‑share pricing; European BaaS TAM heading toward ~$200B+ mid‑decade[9][10][26].
  • Internal tool productization: risk, KYC/AML, onboarding, fraud, and reporting platforms packaged as SaaS/managed services[11][27].

Operating Model Shifts

Success hinges on API‑first/microservices, strong data governance (privacy‑preserving analytics), and a platform operating model with ecosystem KPIs[11][12][24].

Competitive Positioning

Incumbents can pivot from product competitors to platform providers, monetizing rails and data while enabling fintech front‑ends—capturing fee pools and accelerating innovation[13][14][15].

Socio‑Economic Implications

Positive Effects

Democratized access to advanced tooling, faster innovation diffusion, inclusion gains via platform finance, job creation in data/AI/product, and improved market efficiency through better information flows[5][16][17][18].

Risks

Data privacy/security (GDPR fines hit $1.8B in 2023), platform concentration, digital divides, and new systemic dependencies on platform rails[20][19][21][22]. Mitigate via strong data ethics, openness, and supervisory engagement[23][24].

European Outlook

Regulatory Environment

Europe’s PSD2→PSD3 momentum and GDPR underpin API ecosystems and responsible data monetization; API‑based transactions keep rising (~+35%) and harmonization should reduce cross‑border friction by 2026[25][26][20].

Banks’ Approach & Products

Banks are shifting toward horizontal, shared capabilities and platform plays, planning to expose ~half of APIs to partners/public within 3 years[27][28][29]. European BaaS champions include Solaris, Treezor, and Bankable[30].

Neobanks

N26 (10M+), Revolut (tool commercialization), and bunq (values‑led) illustrate horizontal expansion and platform monetization at scale[31][3].

Worldwide Outlook

Market Dynamics

Global BaaS is scaling from $24.6B (2025) toward $60B+ (2030); APAC grows fastest (~23% CAGR), NA leads share, Europe remains largest market[26][6].

Global Bank Playbooks

JPMorgan (AI/ML personalization), Capital One (all‑in public cloud), and BBVA (Open Platform) showcase platform‑first operating models and capability monetization[32][6].

International Enablers

Marqeta, Green Dot, Cross River and others provide rails for card issuing, compliance, and cross‑border—accelerating horizontal productization[6][26]. Revolut’s rapid global expansion demonstrates API‑first leverage[33].

Challenges

Cross‑border compliance, resilient cloud architectures, and local partnership networks are essential for scale[34][35][36].

CEE Strategy & Recommendations

Market Assessment

Only ~36% of CEE FIs have clear digital strategies; yet ~89% expect disruption and ~60% anticipate significant change—an opening for platform plays under EU regulatory umbrellas[17][37].

What to Do Now (0–12 months)

  • Capability inventory: shortlist internal tools (risk, KYC/AML, onboarding, reporting, analytics) for external pilots[39][42].
  • API foundations: migrate to API‑first/microservices; stand up developer portal, auth, billing, and observability[11][58][95].
  • Pilot commercialization: limited cohorts, tiered pricing, and clear SLAs; copy Revolut’s waitlist pattern to de‑risk[3].

Build the Platform (1–3 years)

  • Aggregate capabilities: unify multiple tools behind one platform brand; monetize APIs and dashboards[9][29].
  • Partnerships: fintech/ISV alliances and bank‑to‑bank distribution to compress CAC[10][30].
  • Data governance: privacy‑preserving analytics, robust consent, and GDPR‑grade controls[20][24].

Scale & Lead (3–5 years)

  • Regional platform provider: export BaaS and capability‑as‑a‑service across CEE via EU passporting[37][68].
  • Innovation hubs: institutionalize venture building and spin‑outs; fund via platform revenues[12].

Risk Mitigation & KPIs

Risks: regulatory (fragmentation), cyber/privacy, talent gaps. Mitigants: proactive regulatory liaisons, zero‑trust/cloud security, academy programs[38][17]. KPIs: % revenue from new streams (target 5–10% in 3y), API TPS/uptime (≥99.9%), partner count, monetized API share, external client NPS[59][29].

Investment Envelope

Plan for ~2–4% of revenues over 3 years across platform modernization (40–50%), talent (25–30%), compliance/security (15–20%), and GTM (10–15%)—with upside from 1–2% incremental revenue in base cases and double‑digit potential in breakout scenarios[12][55].

Conclusion

Monetizing internal capabilities is a pragmatic path to growth, resilience, and ecosystem leadership. Start with what already works, productize it behind secure APIs, and scale horizontally—then compound the gains by reinvesting into the platform.
