Security Monetization in Banking: Deep Dive Research

This analysis examines how banks monetize security capabilities across KYC‑as‑a‑Service, biometric authentication, fraud prevention, and compliance‑as‑a‑service, and the strategic, operational, and regulatory implications in Europe, worldwide, and in CEE.

Deep Overview

Market Landscape and Current Trends

The global banking security market was ~$64.0B in 2023 and is projected to reach $146.2B by 2030 (CAGR ~12.9%)[1]. Within this, biometrics is a major growth engine: the market is estimated at $47.2B in 2024 rising to $84.5B by 2029 (CAGR 12.3%)[2]. Voice biometrics alone is valued at $2.39B in 2025 and forecast to reach $7.01B by 2032 (CAGR ~16.6%)[3][43][55][46].

Banks are turning security into revenue through: (1) KYC‑as‑a‑Service; (2) biometric auth (face/voice/fingerprint); (3) fraud prevention APIs; and (4) compliance‑as‑a‑service (AML/CTF, perpetual KYC)[27][22][45].

Revenue Models and Monetization

Subscriptions (e.g., identity‑verification platforms ~$299–$1,199/mo)[4]; per‑check pricing (~$0.50–$1.85 per verification, with common prices around $1.35–$1.40)[5][6][7]; revenue‑sharing in BaaS ecosystems (global BaaS revenue projected at $94B by 2028)[8]; and tiered/enterprise pricing for regulated features[32][36].

Implications for Banks

Strategic Positioning (Build vs Buy vs Partner)

Building in‑house maximizes control/margins but is capital‑intensive—annual KYC reviews can cost $60–$175M at institutional banks[9]. Buying accelerates time‑to‑market (critical as 67% of banks report losing clients due to slow KYC)[9]. Partnerships (white‑label/BaaS) balance speed, distribution, and capex[8][27].

Operational Challenges

Legacy integration: ~55% of FIs cite legacy tech as a top barrier to digital transformation[10]. Regulatory change: the EU AI Act (effective 2024; transparency deadlines by 2026) impacts model governance and disclosures[11][15]. UX trade‑offs: consumers overwhelmingly prefer biometrics to passwords (~93%) but change management is non‑trivial[12][31].

Revenue Opportunities

Direct security services (KYC/AML/CTF), white‑label security tech, anonymized threat‑intel (within privacy laws), and cross‑selling powered by stronger risk signals and smoother onboarding[27][21][39].

Socio‑Economic Implications

Financial Inclusion

Positive: biometrics and remote KYC expand access where documents/branches are scarce; lower unit costs can mean cheaper products[12][57]. Risks: cost pass‑throughs, complex checks excluding vulnerable users, and privacy concerns dampening adoption[60][65].

Employment & Skills

Manual reviews decline while AI/ML, cyber, and reg‑tech roles grow; ~35% of banks cite a skilled‑workforce shortage as a major obstacle[13][10].

Privacy & Surveillance

Identity theft is implicated in a large share of fraud (e.g., ~40% in Europe)[14]. Biometric data is highly sensitive and often regulated under GDPR/BIPA; breach impacts are irreversible, requiring strong template protection and governance[31][59].

European Outlook

Regulatory Situation

The EU stack now includes PSR (successor to PSD2; SCA & API clarity), the EU AI Act, GDPR, and DORA for operational resilience—together shaping how banks build, monetize, and supervise security services[15][11].

European Banks & Products

Case highlights: HSBC voice biometrics at scale (15M+ customers; fraud reduced), Deutsche Bank hardware‑based auth for premium segments (fraud reductions), Nordea with faster logins and fewer password issues—illustrating ROI alongside CX gains[3][16]. Common offerings: MFA, continuous authentication, and API‑first platforms for B2B monetization[20][23].

Neobanks

Revolut/N26/bunq emphasize biometric onboarding, jailbreak/root detection, behavioral analytics, perpetual KYC, and verification‑of‑payee—often exposing security via APIs to businesses[58][17][70]. EU passporting and low interchange push monetization toward subscriptions and security value‑add[67].

Worldwide Outlook

Global Market Situation

North America leads voice biometrics (~36.8% share), APAC grows fastest on digital payments and inclusion mandates, and emerging markets benefit from mobile‑first rails and fewer legacy constraints[3][83].

Banks’ Products & Approaches

U.S. banks deploy multimodal biometrics and AI fraud stacks; Asian banks (e.g., ICICI, HSBC China, Seven Bank) lead in voice/facial/ATM biometrics[20][28]. Architecture trends: API‑first, AI‑powered detection, and cross‑border operability[41][21].

Worldwide Neobank Strategy

U.S. neobanks monetize via partnerships and interchange; Brazil’s Nubank shows scale via superior UX and national instant‑payment rails (Pix)[93]. Scaling playbooks: multi‑regional compliance, cultural localization of auth preferences, and local partner networks[64][63].

CEE Strategy and Recommendations

Current CEE Landscape

CEE banking expects significant disruption; top obstacles include security breaches (~45%), management readiness (~39%), and skills (~35%). Digital banking penetration expected to exceed 65% by 2025; VC activity and EU alignment aid scale‑out[13][18][92].

Recommendations

  • Security‑first entry: build multi‑modal biometrics, perpetual KYC, and API‑first platforms; target SMEs, cross‑border EU corridors, and inclusion segments[22][70].
  • Partnership monetization: B2B2C with incumbents; KYCaaS for fintechs; launch BaaS modules[8][27].
  • Cloud‑first & AI: microservices, containerization, behavioral analytics, NLP, predictive risk; zero‑trust, E2E encryption, biometric template protection[34][21][31].
  • Compliance stack: align with AI Act, GDPR, PSR; automate reporting/monitoring; robust audit trails[11][15][72].
  • Phased scale‑up: 1) licenses & platform; 2) launch in PL/CZ/EE with B2B focus; 3) expand to more CEE & consumer; 4) optimize and pursue M&A/exit options[95].
  • Funding & risk: raise €5–10M seed for build/compliance; €15–25M Series A for entry; €50–100M growth for regionalization; mitigate reg/tech/competitive risks via regulator engagement, differentiation, and strong cyber controls[10][54].

Conclusion

With EU regulation maturing and demand rising, CEE banks can monetize security by productizing identity, biometrics, fraud, and compliance. Winners will pair best‑in‑class security with great UX, API platforms, and partnership distribution.
